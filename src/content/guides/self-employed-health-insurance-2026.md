## Marketplace Coverage for Freelancers and Gig Workers

The ACA marketplace is the primary source of individual health insurance for self-employed workers, freelancers, independent contractors, and gig economy participants. Unlike employer-sponsored coverage, marketplace plans are purchased directly, and the full premium is the individual's responsibility. However, premium tax credits based on income can reduce costs substantially.

Self-employed individuals apply through Healthcare.gov or their state exchange during open enrollment or during a special enrollment period triggered by a qualifying event. The application process requires an estimate of annual household income, which determines the advance premium tax credit applied monthly to reduce the plan premium.

## Managing Variable Income

The most significant challenge for self-employed marketplace enrollees is accurately projecting annual income. Freelance and gig income fluctuates monthly, and the subsidy is calculated on projected annual MAGI. Overestimating income results in a smaller advance credit (and a refund at tax time), while underestimating results in a larger advance credit that may need to be partially repaid when filing taxes.

Best practices include basing projections on the most recent tax return, adjusting for known changes in contracts or business conditions, and updating the marketplace estimate mid-year if income deviates significantly from the original projection. The marketplace allows income updates at any time, which adjusts the monthly premium credit going forward.

## The Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction

Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of health insurance premiums paid for themselves and their dependents as an above-the-line deduction on their federal tax return (IRS Form 1040, Schedule 1). This deduction reduces adjusted gross income, which can in turn increase ACA subsidy eligibility in a circular calculation.

The deduction applies to the net premium after subtracting the advance premium tax credit. For example, if the full Silver plan premium is $600 per month and the advance credit covers $400, the deductible amount is $200 per month ($2,400 annually). This deduction is available regardless of whether the self-employed individual itemizes deductions.

## HSA Strategy for Self-Employed Workers

Self-employed individuals enrolled in HSA-eligible high-deductible health plans (HDHPs) can contribute to a Health Savings Account, gaining a triple tax advantage: contributions are tax-deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. For 2026, individual HSA contribution limits are projected at approximately $4,300, and family limits at approximately $8,550.

Pairing a Bronze HDHP with an HSA allows self-employed workers to maintain low monthly premiums while building a tax-advantaged fund for medical expenses. HSA contributions also reduce MAGI, potentially increasing ACA subsidy eligibility. This strategy works best for relatively healthy individuals who can tolerate the higher deductible in exchange for premium savings and tax benefits.

## Choosing Between Plan Tiers

For self-employed workers with predictable, moderate income, Silver plans with cost-sharing reductions (for those below 250% FPL) typically offer the best value. For higher earners with minimal healthcare needs, a Bronze HDHP with HSA provides the lowest premium and tax optimization. Gold plans suit self-employed individuals with ongoing treatment needs or expensive prescriptions where the lower cost-sharing offsets the higher premium.
