## How Medicaid and Marketplace Coverage Differ

Medicaid is a joint federal and state program that provides health coverage to eligible low-income individuals and families. ACA marketplace plans are private health insurance policies available through Healthcare.gov or state exchanges, with federal subsidies that reduce premiums based on income. The fundamental difference is cost structure: Medicaid has minimal out-of-pocket expenses, while marketplace plans involve monthly premiums, deductibles, copays, and coinsurance even with financial assistance.

In states that have expanded Medicaid under the ACA, adults with household income up to 138% of the Federal Poverty Level generally qualify for Medicaid. Above that threshold, individuals typically transition to marketplace plan eligibility with premium tax credits. In non-expansion states, the income threshold for Medicaid remains much lower, and a coverage gap may exist for adults earning above traditional Medicaid limits but below 100% FPL.

## Eligibility and Income Thresholds

For a single adult in 2026, 138% of the Federal Poverty Level is approximately $21,597 in expansion states. Marketplace premium tax credits generally begin at 100% FPL (approximately $15,650 for a single adult) and extend through all income levels under current enhanced subsidy rules, with the maximum expected contribution capped at 8.5% of household income.

Household size significantly affects both programs. A family of four with income below approximately $44,367 may qualify for Medicaid in expansion states, while marketplace subsidies would apply above that level. Income is measured as Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI), which includes wages, self-employment income, Social Security benefits, and certain other income sources.

## Choosing Between Programs

For individuals near the income boundary, the choice between Medicaid and marketplace coverage often depends on income stability, provider preferences, and healthcare needs. Those with predictable income below the Medicaid threshold benefit from the program's minimal cost-sharing. Those with income that may fluctuate above the threshold should consider the implications of mid-year transitions between programs.

Marketplace Silver plans with cost-sharing reductions offer a middle ground for those with income between 100% and 250% FPL, providing reduced deductibles and copays that approach Medicaid-level cost-sharing while maintaining access to broader provider networks.

## The Medicaid Coverage Gap

In states that have not expanded Medicaid, adults without children or with income above very low traditional thresholds may fall into a coverage gap where they earn too much for Medicaid but too little to qualify for marketplace subsidies. Individuals in this situation should check whether their state has plans to expand Medicaid or explore other coverage options such as community health centers, short-term plans, or healthcare sharing ministries.
