## What Is the ACA Subsidy Cliff?

The Affordable Care Act originally structured premium tax credits with a hard income cutoff at 400% of the Federal Poverty Level. Below this threshold, the government limits how much a household is expected to pay toward a benchmark Silver plan premium, with the remaining cost covered by a tax credit. Above 400% FPL, the credit drops to zero, creating what is known as the subsidy cliff.

For a single adult in 2026, 400% FPL is approximately $62,600. Under the original ACA rules, a person earning $62,000 might pay roughly $440 per month for a Silver plan, while someone earning $63,000 would pay the full unsubsidized premium, potentially $600 or more per month. This abrupt transition created significant financial pressure for middle-income individuals and families.

## How Enhanced Subsidies Changed the Landscape

The American Rescue Plan Act of 2021 and the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 temporarily enhanced ACA subsidies by eliminating the 400% FPL cliff and capping the maximum premium contribution at 8.5% of household income for all marketplace enrollees. Under these enhanced rules, no marketplace enrollee is expected to spend more than 8.5% of income on a benchmark Silver plan premium, regardless of income level.

This change provided relief to middle-income households that previously fell just above the cliff. A single adult earning $70,000 per year, for example, would be expected to pay no more than approximately $496 per month toward a benchmark plan, with any remaining premium covered by the tax credit.

## The 2026 Uncertainty

The enhanced subsidy provisions were set to expire at the end of 2025. Congressional action determines whether these provisions are extended, modified, or allowed to lapse. If the enhanced subsidies expire, the original cliff at 400% FPL returns, and marketplace premiums for middle-income enrollees could increase substantially.

Marketplace enrollees with income above 400% FPL should prepare for the possibility that their premium tax credits could be reduced or eliminated. Those near the threshold should understand the exact income levels at which the cliff applies for their household size.

## Strategies for Managing Income Near the Cliff

For individuals with some control over their reported income, certain strategies may help manage exposure to the subsidy cliff. Traditional IRA contributions, HSA contributions (for those with eligible high-deductible health plans), and self-employed retirement plan contributions reduce Modified Adjusted Gross Income, potentially keeping income below critical thresholds.

These strategies are not available or practical for all households, and they should never compromise long-term financial health solely for subsidy purposes. Consulting a qualified tax professional before making income-related decisions based on ACA subsidy thresholds is strongly recommended.

## Monitoring Legislative Changes

The status of enhanced subsidies is subject to Congressional action and may change with limited advance notice. Marketplace enrollees should monitor updates from Healthcare.gov, state exchange websites, and reputable health policy organizations such as the Kaiser Family Foundation for the latest information on subsidy rules and income thresholds.
