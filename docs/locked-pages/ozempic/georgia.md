## 15. SECTION: WHEN COMPARING PLANS FOR OZEMPIC, CHECK THESE IN ORDER

**WHEN COMPARING PLANS FOR OZEMPIC, CHECK THESE IN ORDER**

**1. Is Ozempic on your plan's drug list?**
Every Georgia marketplace plan for 2026 lists Ozempic — all 324 of them. Still, confirm your specific plan's drug list before you enroll, as coverage terms and tiers can change.

**2. What tier is it on?**
The tier determines what you pay after your deductible. Georgia insurance companies place Ozempic on their lower-cost brand tier. Check the Ozempic copay on the specific plan you are considering before you enroll.

**3. Does your deductible apply to prescriptions?**
Most plans require you to meet a deductible before the lower copay kicks in. Some plans have a separate, smaller deductible just for prescriptions — which means you reach the lower copay sooner. Others combine drug and medical costs into one deductible, so you may pay the pre-deductible price longer.

**4. Does your plan require approval — or require you to try a different drug first?**
Most Georgia marketplace plans require approval before you can fill Ozempic (called prior authorization). Some plans also require you to try a different drug before they will cover Ozempic (called step therapy). Both rules can delay your first fill. Check the plan you are considering for both.

If you take Ozempic, a plan with a lower drug tier, a lower deductible, and no step therapy requirement will usually cost you less and start sooner — even if the monthly premium is slightly higher.

## 16. SECTION: WHY OZEMPIC COSTS MORE TO COVER THAN MANY DRUGS

**WHY OZEMPIC COSTS MORE TO COVER THAN MANY DRUGS**

Ozempic is a brand-name injectable, not a generic. Georgia insurance companies cover it, and most require approval before you can fill it — which means you may pay more out of pocket and wait longer to start than you would for a common generic like metformin or lisinopril. Some plans also require you to try a different drug first. If the cost or approval rules are a problem, ask your doctor about Rybelsus — same active ingredient, just in pill form — or Trulicity. Your plan may put those on a cheaper tier or skip the step therapy requirement.

## 19. SECTION: IF IT IS TAKING TOO LONG OR YOUR PLAN DENIES COVERAGE

**IF IT IS TAKING TOO LONG OR YOUR PLAN DENIES COVERAGE**

**1. Call your doctor's office for an update**
If you have been waiting longer than a few days and have not heard anything, call your doctor's office. They may be able to tell you whether the approval is still pending.

**2. If denied, your doctor may be able to ask for another review directly**
Your doctor may be able to ask for another review directly. This is sometimes called a peer-to-peer review. It is not offered by every plan, so ask your doctor's office early whether this is an option.

**3. File a formal appeal — or switch plans at the next open enrollment**
If the denial stands, your plan will send you a notice explaining why and how to appeal. Check that notice closely — it will include the deadline and the steps to follow. If Ozempic coverage continues to be a problem, open enrollment is your chance to switch to a different plan.

## 23. FAQ SECTION

**COMMON QUESTIONS ABOUT OZEMPIC COVERAGE IN GEORGIA**

**Q: Can I switch plans mid-year to get better Ozempic coverage?**
In most cases, no. You can only switch mid-year if you have a major life change — like losing other coverage, getting married, or having a baby. If you are planning to switch at the next open enrollment, check the new plan's Ozempic tier, whether it requires approval or step therapy, and what you would likely pay after your deductible.

**Q: What is the difference between prior authorization and a coverage exception?**
Prior authorization is approval for a drug that is on your plan's drug list. A coverage exception is a request to cover a drug that is not on the list, or to move it to a lower-cost tier. Your doctor files both. The plan reviews each one against its own rules.

**Q: What is step therapy and how does it work for Ozempic in Georgia?**
Step therapy means your plan requires you to try one or more other drugs before it will cover Ozempic. Some Georgia plans require this, others do not. If your plan requires step therapy, your doctor can request an exception with documentation explaining why the other drug is not appropriate for you.

**Q: Will my plan tell me if Ozempic's coverage changes during the year?**
Yes. Plans send a written notice if they remove a drug, change its tier, or add new approval rules. The notice usually arrives by mail or through your plan's member portal. If you take Ozempic regularly, watch for these notices and check your plan's drug list a few weeks before each refill.

**Q: If Ozempic is denied, is Wegovy a covered alternative?**
Wegovy and Ozempic share the same active ingredient but are approved for different uses — Ozempic for type 2 diabetes, Wegovy for weight management. Coverage rules differ. Check your plan's drug list for Wegovy specifically before assuming it is an alternative. See how Wegovy is covered in Georgia.

**Q: Does every Affordable Care Act (ACA) plan in Georgia have to cover Ozempic?**
No. The ACA requires plans to cover prescription drugs as an essential health benefit, but each plan decides which specific drugs to include on its drug list. All 324 Georgia marketplace plans for 2026 happen to cover Ozempic, but coverage is not federally required for any specific brand-name drug.
