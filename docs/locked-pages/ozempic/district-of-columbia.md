## 16. SECTION: WHY OZEMPIC COSTS MORE TO COVER THAN MANY DRUGS

**WHY OZEMPIC COSTS MORE TO COVER THAN MANY DRUGS**

Ozempic is a brand-name injectable, not a generic. All three District of Columbia insurance companies cover it, but most place it on a non-preferred brand tier rather than a preferred brand tier — and all require approval before you can fill it. That combination means you may pay more out of pocket and wait longer to start than you would for a common generic like metformin or lisinopril. If the cost is a problem, ask your doctor about Rybelsus — same active ingredient, just in pill form — or Trulicity. Your plan may put those on a cheaper tier.

**IF IT IS TAKING TOO LONG OR YOUR PLAN DENIES COVERAGE**

**1. Call your doctor's office for an update**
If you have been waiting longer than a few days and have not heard anything, call your doctor's office. They may be able to tell you whether the approval is still pending.

## 23. FAQ SECTION

**COMMON QUESTIONS ABOUT OZEMPIC COVERAGE IN THE DISTRICT OF COLUMBIA**

**Q: Why is Ozempic on a non-preferred tier on most District of Columbia plans?**
Insurance companies decide tier placement based on contracts they negotiate with drug manufacturers and pharmacy benefit managers. Most insurance companies in the District of Columbia place Ozempic on a non-preferred brand tier in 2026, which usually costs more than a preferred brand tier. One uses a more favorable tier. The tier label affects what you pay after your deductible.

**Q: Can I switch plans mid-year to get better Ozempic coverage?**
In most cases, no. You can only switch mid-year if you have a major life change — like losing other coverage, getting married, or having a baby. If you are planning to switch at the next open enrollment, check the new plan's Ozempic tier first — the tier is the biggest cost factor in the District.

**Q: What is the difference between prior authorization and a coverage exception?**
Prior authorization is approval your plan requires before it will cover a drug that is on its drug list. A coverage exception is a request to cover a drug that is not on your plan's drug list, or to move it to a lower-cost tier. Both are filed by your doctor with documentation. The plan reviews each one against its own rules.

**Q: Can I request a tier exception to lower the cost of Ozempic?**
Some plans allow tier exceptions — your doctor submits a request asking the plan to treat Ozempic as if it were on a lower-cost tier. Plans review these case by case. It is worth asking about, especially in the District of Columbia where most plans use a non-preferred brand tier for Ozempic.

**Q: If Ozempic is denied, is Wegovy a covered alternative?**
Wegovy and Ozempic share the same active ingredient but are approved for different uses — Ozempic for type 2 diabetes, Wegovy for weight management. Coverage rules differ. Check your plan's drug list for Wegovy specifically before assuming it is an alternative. See how Wegovy is covered in the District of Columbia.

**Q: Does every Affordable Care Act (ACA) plan in the District of Columbia have to cover Ozempic?**
No. The ACA requires plans to cover prescription drugs as an essential health benefit, but each plan decides which specific drugs to include on its drug list. All 94 District of Columbia marketplace plans for 2026 happen to cover Ozempic, but coverage is not federally required for any specific brand-name drug.
