The drug tier is the single biggest factor in what you pay for Ozempic. Every plan sorts drugs into cost levels called tiers — and two plans from the same insurance company can put Ozempic on different tiers, which means very different costs for you. When you compare plans, check what tier each one assigns to Ozempic first.

Drug tier This affects your cost more than anything else. A preferred brand tier means lower copays after your deductible. A specialty or non-preferred tier can cost two to three times more per month.
Prescription deductible Some plans have a separate deductible just for prescriptions — usually smaller than the medical deductible, so you reach the lower copay sooner. When comparing plans for Ozempic, check these in order

1
Is Ozempic on your plan's drug list?
Do not assume every plan covers it. Five of the 52 North Carolina plans we reviewed do not list Ozempic at all. If Ozempic is not on your plan's drug list, you would pay full price or need to find a covered alternative.
2
What tier is it on?
The tier determines what you pay after your deductible. A preferred brand tier means lower copays. A specialty or non-preferred tier can cost two to three times more per month.
3
Does your deductible apply to prescriptions?
Most plans require you to meet a deductible before the lower copay kicks in. Some plans have a separate, smaller deductible just for prescriptions — which means you reach the lower copay sooner. Others combine drug and medical costs into one deductible, so you may pay the higher amount longer.
4
Does your plan require approval before you can fill it?
On most plans, you cannot fill Ozempic until your plan approves it first (called prior authorization). That can add several days to your first fill. If you are denied, you can appeal.

If you take Ozempic, a plan with a lower drug tier and a lower deductible will usually cost you less over the year — even if the monthly premium is slightly higher.

Why Ozempic costs more to cover than many drugs

Ozempic is a brand-name injectable, not a generic. Most North Carolina plans put it on a higher-cost tier and will usually require approval before you can fill it — which means you may pay more out of pocket and wait longer to start than you would for a common generic like metformin or lisinopril. If the cost is a problem, ask your doctor about Rybelsus — same active ingredient, just in pill form — or Trulicity. Your plan may put those on a cheaper tier.

What to expect while you wait

At the pharmacy
You may find out approval is needed when you try to pick it up
Your pharmacist can tell you on the spot whether your plan needs to approve Ozempic first. If it does, the pharmacy will send the request to your doctor. You will not be able to fill it until your plan signs off.
The wait
A few days is common, but it can take longer
Some approvals come back in a day or two. Others take a week or more, especially if your plan asks your doctor for more details. If you need the medication soon, ask your doctor to mark the request urgent — urgent reviews generally move faster.

If it is taking too long or your plan denies coverage

1
Call your doctor's office for an update
If you have been waiting longer than a few days and have not heard anything, call your doctor's office. They can check what is holding things up and whether anything else is needed.
2
If denied, ask your doctor to request a peer-to-peer review
A peer-to-peer review puts your doctor on a call with your plan's medical reviewer. It can be faster than a formal appeal and gives your doctor a direct chance to explain why Ozempic is the right drug for you — not an alternative.
3
File a formal appeal — or switch plans at the next open enrollment
If the denial stands, your plan will send you a notice explaining why and how to appeal. Check that notice closely — it will include the deadline and the steps to follow. If Ozempic coverage continues to be a problem, open enrollment is your chance to switch to a different plan.

Common questions about Ozempic coverage in North Carolina

Can I switch plans mid-year to get better Ozempic coverage?▼
In most cases, no. You can only switch mid-year if you have a major life change — like losing other coverage, getting married, or having a baby. If you are planning to switch at the next open enrollment, check the new plan's Ozempic tier, whether it requires approval, and what you would likely pay after your deductible. In the meantime, ask your doctor whether a different covered drug might work for your situation.
What is the difference between prior authorization and a coverage exception?▼
Prior authorization (sometimes called "prior auth") means you need approval before you can pick up the prescription at the pharmacy. A coverage exception is when you ask your plan to cover a drug that is not on its standard drug list, or to move it to a cheaper tier. They solve different problems — prior authorization affects when you can fill it, while a coverage exception affects whether your plan covers it at all.
Will my plan tell me if Ozempic's coverage changes during the year?▼
Yes. Your plan must give you at least 60 days' notice before most mid-year changes take effect — a tier move, a new approval requirement, or Ozempic being removed from the list. The notice will explain what is changing and when. Read it carefully, because the deadlines for requesting a coverage exception or appealing a change are short.
If Ozempic is denied, is Wegovy a covered alternative?▼
Not automatically. Wegovy is a separate drug with its own tier, cost, and approval rules. Ozempic is approved for type 2 diabetes, while Wegovy is approved for weight management. Your plan may only cover each drug for its approved use — so even though they have the same active ingredient, your coverage for each one may be very different. See how Wegovy is covered in North Carolina.
Does every ACA plan in North Carolina have to cover Ozempic?▼
No. ACA plans must cover at least one drug in each drug category, but they choose which specific drugs to include. Five of the 52 North Carolina plans we reviewed do not cover Ozempic at all. If your plan does not cover it, you can request a coverage exception, ask your doctor about a different drug your plan does cover, or compare plans that cover Ozempic during open enrollment.
Can Ozempic coverage change after I enroll in a plan?▼
Yes. Your plan can move drugs to different tiers, add approval requirements, or remove drugs from the drug list during the year. For most changes, you will get at least 60 days' notice. If Ozempic becomes more expensive or harder to get on your plan, you can compare other plans at the next open enrollment.
