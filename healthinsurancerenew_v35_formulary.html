How the approval process typically works

Day 1
Your doctor submits the request
Your physician sends your diagnosis and the required medical information. You do not need to file anything directly with your insurance company.
Days 1–3
Request under review
Most standard approvals are decided within a few business days. Urgent requests may be resolved faster. Check your plan benefits for the specific timeline.
After approval
The prescription can be filled
Once approved, your pharmacy can fill the prescription. What you pay at the counter depends on Ozempic's tier and whether your deductible is met.

If your request is delayed or denied

1
Confirm the request was submitted and received
Contact your doctor's office to verify that all required documentation was sent and received by your insurance company. An incomplete or missing submission is the most common reason prescriptions are turned away at the pharmacy.
2
Ask for a peer-to-peer review
Your doctor's office can request this directly with the insurance company. It often resolves a denial faster than a formal appeal.
3
File a formal appeal — or compare plans at open enrollment
Insurance companies are required to respond to standard appeals within 30 days. If getting coverage for this drug continues to be a problem, open enrollment is the right time to switch to a plan that covers it better.

Common questions about Ozempic coverage in North Carolina

Can I switch plans mid-year to get better Ozempic coverage?▼
In most cases, no. Plan changes outside of open enrollment (November through January in North Carolina) require a qualifying life event — such as losing other coverage, getting married, or having a child. Drug coverage terms reset each January, so if you are switching plans, verify Ozempic's tier on the new plan before you enroll.
What is the difference between insurance approval and a coverage exception?▼
Insurance approval (sometimes called prior authorization) confirms the drug is covered under your plan before your first fill. A coverage exception is a separate request — it asks the plan either to cover a drug not currently on their list, or to move it to a lower tier. Both require documentation from your doctor, but they address different problems.
What happens if my plan changes Ozempic's coverage during the year?▼
Plans can revise drug lists and tier placements during the year. For non-urgent changes, most plans are required to give 60 days' notice. If your plan moves Ozempic to a higher tier or removes it entirely, that change may qualify you for a special enrollment period. Review your plan's benefit documents for the notice and transition rules specific to your coverage.
If Ozempic is denied, is Wegovy a covered alternative?▼
Not automatically. Wegovy is a separate drug with its own coverage rules — your plan may treat it very differently from Ozempic. Coverage also depends on the clinical indication: Ozempic is approved for type 2 diabetes management, while Wegovy is approved for weight management. Your doctor would need to confirm it is appropriate for your situation, and you would still need to go through your plan's approval process. See how Wegovy is covered in North Carolina.
Does every ACA plan in North Carolina have to cover Ozempic?▼
No. ACA plans must cover at least one drug in each therapeutic category, but they are not required to include every brand. Five of the 52 North Carolina plans we reviewed did not list Ozempic at all. If your plan does not include it, you can request a coverage exception or compare plans that do during open enrollment.
Can Ozempic coverage change after I enroll in a plan?▼
Yes. Plans can update their drug list, change tier placements, or add new approval requirements during the plan year. For non-urgent changes, most plans must give at least 60 days' notice. If a change affects your access to Ozempic, it may qualify you for a special enrollment period to switch plans.
