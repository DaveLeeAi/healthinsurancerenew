## What Is the ACA Subsidy Cliff?

The Affordable Care Act originally structured premium tax credits with a hard income cutoff at 400% of the Federal Poverty Level. Below this threshold, the government limits how much a household is expected to pay toward a benchmark Silver plan premium, with the remaining cost covered by a tax credit. Above 400% FPL, the credit drops to zero, creating what is known as the subsidy cliff.

For a single adult in 2026, 400% FPL is approximately $62,600. Under the original ACA rules, a person earning $62,000 might pay roughly $440 per month for a Silver plan, while someone earning $63,000 would pay the full unsubsidized premium, potentially $600 or more per month. This abrupt transition created significant financial pressure for middle-income individuals and families.

## How Enhanced Subsidies Changed the Landscape (2021–2025)

The American Rescue Plan Act of 2021 and the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 temporarily enhanced ACA subsidies by eliminating the 400% FPL cliff and capping the maximum premium contribution at 8.5% of household income for all marketplace enrollees. Under those enhanced rules, no marketplace enrollee was expected to spend more than 8.5% of income on a benchmark Silver plan premium, regardless of income level.

This change provided relief to middle-income households that previously fell just above the cliff. A single adult earning $70,000 per year, for example, paid no more than approximately $496 per month toward a benchmark plan, with any remaining premium covered by the tax credit.

## What Changed for 2026

The enhanced subsidy provisions expired on December 31, 2025. No extension was signed into law. The original cliff at 400% FPL has returned, and marketplace premiums for middle-income enrollees above that threshold have increased substantially.

Marketplace enrollees with income above 400% FPL now receive zero premium tax credits. Those near the threshold should understand the exact income levels at which the cliff applies for their household size: $62,600 (single), $84,880 (family of 2), $107,160 (family of 3), or $128,600 (family of 4).

## Strategies for Managing Income Near the Cliff

For individuals with some control over their reported income, certain strategies may help manage exposure to the subsidy cliff. Traditional IRA contributions, HSA contributions (for those with eligible high-deductible health plans), and self-employed retirement plan contributions reduce Modified Adjusted Gross Income, potentially keeping income below critical thresholds.

These strategies are not available or practical for all households, and they should never compromise long-term financial health solely for subsidy purposes. Consulting a qualified tax professional before making income-related decisions based on ACA subsidy thresholds is strongly recommended.

## Monitoring Legislative Changes

Congress could act to reinstate enhanced subsidies in the future, but as of 2026 the standard rules are in effect. Marketplace enrollees should monitor updates from Healthcare.gov, state exchange websites, and reputable health policy organizations such as the Kaiser Family Foundation for the latest information on subsidy rules and income thresholds.
